# Positioning Statement Worksheet

Build the post-acquisition positioning statement. Customer-outcome framed, one paragraph, no buzzwords.

## The structured questions

### 1. Who is the customer?

[Persona, company size, role, the problem they own. Be specific.]

### 2. What can they do or achieve now that they could not before?

[The new capability the combination unlocks. Frame as a customer outcome. If you cannot answer this in two sentences, the deal thesis is not yet sharp enough for external messaging.]

### 3. Why does this matter to them?

[The business outcome. Time saved, revenue protected, risk reduced, capability unlocked. Quantify if you can.]

### 4. What were they doing before?

[Workarounds, multiple tools, manual work, accepting limits. Names the pain you are solving.]

### 5. What does it not do?

[The boundaries. Naming what is out of scope is what makes the in-scope claim credible.]

## Draft positioning statement

Combine the answers into a single paragraph. No buzzwords. No filler. The kind of paragraph a sales rep can repeat without changing a word.

> [Customer persona] can now [new capability] without [previous workaround], so they can [business outcome]. Before, this required [previous state]. The combined [acquirer] and [acquired] product enables [specific capability], with the same [trust signals: security, support, etc.] customers have always relied on.

## Tests

- [ ] A sales rep can deliver this on a customer call without improvising
- [ ] It names a customer outcome, not a feature list
- [ ] The acquired team's CEO would recognize their company in this language
- [ ] A CxO at the customer would not roll their eyes
- [ ] A new account executive on day one can hold this paragraph in their head
