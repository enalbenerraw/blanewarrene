# Retention Risk Review (Purpose and Influence)

Per-person review focused on three drivers, not compensation. Run at Day 30, Day 60, Day 90, and quarterly thereafter.

## The three drivers

### Purpose

Do they understand and believe in the combined product mission? Could they explain the deal thesis to a friend in plain English? Do they see how their work contributes to the mission?

### Influence

Are they shaping outcomes, or being shaped by them? When they disagree, does anything change? Have they been named publicly as the source of an outcome that changed?

### Ownership

Do they have something they can point to that is theirs? Is the scope real (not symbolic)? Do they have the authority to actually own it?

## Risk level definitions

- **Green** (all three drivers 4 or 5): Healthy. Maintain.
- **Yellow** (any driver at 3): Watch. Schedule a 1:1 within 7 days. Address the lowest-scoring driver directly.
- **Red** (any driver 2 or below): Acute retention risk regardless of compensation. Senior leader intervention within 48 hours. Build a 30-day remediation plan.

## Conducting the review

Do not survey the person. Score based on direct manager observation, peer feedback, and the person's actual behavior in meetings and decisions. Then have a 1:1 conversation with the person to validate or adjust the scoring.

The conversation is more important than the score. The score creates the obligation to have the conversation.

## Sample remediation actions

- **Low Purpose**: Direct exposure to the executive sponsor's deal thesis conversation. Pair them with a customer who is excited about the combination.
- **Low Influence**: Public acknowledgment of a contribution. Direct ask for their perspective in the next planning session. Visible decision shift driven by their input.
- **Low Ownership**: Hand them a piece of scope with full authority. Remove a layer of approval. Let them communicate the decision to the team themselves.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating the score as confidential when the person is the one who needs to hear it
- Addressing low scores with comp adjustments
- Running the review without acting on the results
- Skipping the conversation because the score was green
