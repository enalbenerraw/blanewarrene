# Conflict Categorization Guide

Use this to decide whether a conflict is Vision, Sequencing, or Scope.

## Vision

A conflict is Vision if any of the following are true:

- The two teams disagree on the primary buyer persona
- The two teams disagree on the core customer outcome
- The two teams have built toward incompatible technical philosophies (for example: monolith vs platform, on-prem vs SaaS, AI-native vs AI-augmented)
- The two teams use the same words to mean different things ("workflow," "automation," "platform" each carrying different definitions)

How to resolve: leadership decision. Cannot be negotiated by PMs or EMs. Communicate the decision in the Combined Product Narrative.

## Sequencing

A conflict is Sequencing if both teams agree on the destination but disagree on order. Common patterns:

- Both teams agree the platform must support multi-tenant; one wants it in Q2, the other in Q4
- Both teams agree an API should ship; one wants it as an MVP, the other wants it complete

How to resolve: customer evidence. Pull support volume, revenue concentration, and named-account requests. Let the data drive the order. Both teams interpret the data together.

## Scope

A conflict is Scope if one team believes a feature, product line, or capability should sunset, merge, or change form. Common patterns:

- Acquirer wants to sunset the acquired company's standalone reporting module
- Acquired team's billing system needs to be replaced by acquirer's
- Two overlapping features need to merge into one

How to resolve: be direct. Make the decision early. Communicate with context. Name the people whose work is changing and explain why. Do not let ambiguity persist.

## When you cannot tell the category

If you cannot decide whether something is Vision or Sequencing, treat it as Vision. The cost of treating a Vision conflict as Sequencing is much higher than the reverse.
