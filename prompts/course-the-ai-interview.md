## 3. Full Lessons

## Lesson 1: The Vending Machine Problem

**Runtime:** ~8 minutes
**Learning objective:** By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the "vending machine" mistake in your current prompts and reframe Claude as a collaborator instead of a command line.

### Lesson Script

Let me start with a question. When was the last time you typed something into Claude — or ChatGPT, or any AI tool — and got back something that was technically correct but completely forgettable? Generic. Bland. The kind of thing you ended up rewriting yourself anyway.

If you're nodding, you're not alone. And you're not doing it wrong, exactly. You're doing what most people do. You're treating Claude like a vending machine.

Here's what I mean. A vending machine works one way. You drop in a request — "B7" — and you get exactly what's behind that slot. Coin in, snack out. Transaction over.

That's how most people prompt Claude. "Write me a marketing email." "Summarize this document." "Give me five social posts." Coin in, snack out, walk away. And the snack is fine. It's just not the meal you actually wanted.

Here's the key idea for this whole course: **Claude isn't a vending machine. Claude is a collaborator.**

Think about how you'd brief a smart new colleague on the same task. You wouldn't just say, "write me a marketing email." You'd say: "Here's the customer we're going after. Here's what we sell. Here's the campaign we ran last month — it bombed. Here's the tone we use. Here's what good looks like. Now let's talk through the angle before we draft anything."

That's collaboration. And that's what changes Claude's output from forgettable to genuinely useful.

So in this course, we're going to learn one pattern. Just one. I call it the **Brief → Ask → Draft → Refine** pattern. Every lesson will reinforce a different piece of it.

- **Brief.** You set the stage with context.
- **Ask.** You let Claude ask *you* clarifying questions before it drafts.
- **Draft.** You produce the first version together.
- **Refine.** You iterate through structured feedback, not by starting over.

Let's see the difference in action. Here's a vending-machine prompt:

```
Write a LinkedIn post about our new pricing.
```

And here's what Claude tends to give back:

```
Excited to announce our new pricing! We've made it simpler,
more affordable, and better aligned with how our customers use
the product. Check out the details on our website and let us
know what you think. #pricing #SaaS #announcement
```

Generic. Hashtag-soup. You've seen this post a thousand times.

Now here's the same task using just the first piece of our pattern — the Brief:

```
You're helping me draft a LinkedIn post.

Context:
- I'm the founder of a 12-person B2B SaaS company that sells
  inventory software to independent retailers.
- We just changed our pricing from a per-seat model to a flat
  $99/month for unlimited seats.
- Our audience is skeptical of SaaS pricing games — they've been
  burned before.
- My voice on LinkedIn is direct, slightly dry, no hype, no emojis.

Goal: A 150-word post announcing the change that earns trust,
not eye-rolls.

Draft it.
```

And here's what Claude tends to give back:

```
Last quarter, three different customers told me the same thing:
"Your per-seat pricing is making me ration who on my team gets to
use the software."

That's not a sales problem. That's a product problem.

So we changed it. Starting today, [Product] is $99/month. Flat.
Unlimited seats. No "growth tier." No surprise charges when your
team grows.

If you've been holding back logins to keep the bill down — stop.
Let your whole team in.

If you've been waiting for us to "figure out" pricing — we did.
This is it.

Same product. Same support. One price.
```

Same model. Same task. The second one sounds like a real human founder. Why?

Because we briefed Claude the way we'd brief a colleague. We named the **audience** (skeptical retailers). We named the **voice** (direct, dry, no hype). We named **what good looks like** (earns trust, not eye-rolls). That technique has a name — it's called *role priming combined with context grounding*. In plain English: tell Claude who it's talking to, in what voice, for what outcome, before you ever say "draft it."

That's it for Lesson 1. The big shift: you're no longer ordering from Claude. You're collaborating with Claude.

In the next lesson, we'll go deep on the Brief — the exact ingredients that turn a vague request into a sharp one. See you there.

## Lesson 2: The Brief — Setting Claude Up for Success

**Runtime:** ~10 minutes
**Learning objective:** By the end of this lesson, you will be able to write a four-part brief that gives Claude the context, audience, voice, and goal it needs to produce sharp output on the first try.

### Lesson Script

Welcome back. Last lesson we established the big shift: you're collaborating with Claude, not ordering from it. Today we're going deep on the first step of our pattern — the **Brief**.

Quick story. A consultant I work with — let's call her Maya — used to spend twenty minutes per client email, even with Claude helping her. She'd type "draft a follow-up email to my client about the project delay," get back something flat, rewrite the whole thing, paste it back, ask for a tone tweak, paste it back again. Twenty minutes per email. Six emails a day. You do the math.

Then she started briefing Claude *once*, the right way, at the start of every email. The drafts went from 20 minutes to 4. Same Claude. Same Maya. The difference was the brief.

So what makes a good brief? Four ingredients. I want you to memorize these.

**One: Context.** Who you are. What the situation is. What just happened. Claude doesn't know your world — you have to hand it the relevant slice.

**Two: Audience.** Who is this *for*? Their role, their relationship to you, their level of skepticism, what they care about.

**Three: Voice.** How *you* sound. Three or four adjectives is enough. "Direct, slightly dry, occasional dry humor, never gushing."

**Four: Goal.** What does success look like? Not the format — the *outcome*. "Get them to agree to a 30-minute call." "Make them feel heard before I deliver the bad news."

Context. Audience. Voice. Goal. CAVG. Write it on a sticky note.

Let's work an example together. Maya's task: draft a follow-up email telling a client that a deliverable is going to be a week late. Vending-machine version:

```
Write an email to my client telling them the project is one
week late.
```

Output: a stiff, corporate "We regret to inform you…" Useless.

Now the briefed version:

```
You're helping me draft a client email.

Context:
- I'm a strategy consultant. The client is the COO of a
  120-person logistics company.
- We're three weeks into a six-week engagement to redesign their
  warehouse routing.
- One key stakeholder — the warehouse manager — has been hard to
  schedule, which is delaying the next deliverable by a week.
- This is the first slip in the project. The relationship is
  good but the COO is detail-oriented and reads everything
  closely.

Audience: The COO. She's busy, skeptical of consultants in
general, and trusts me because I've delivered on time before.

Voice: Direct, no hedging, accountable without being apologetic.
Slight dry humor okay if it lands.

Goal: She finishes the email feeling that I'm in control, that
the slip is contained, and that she doesn't need to escalate to
her CEO.

Draft a 150-word email. Subject line included.
```

And Claude responds with something like:

```
Subject: One-week shift on the routing redesign

Hi Sarah,

Quick update on the warehouse routing work. We're going to land
the next deliverable a week later than planned — May 14 instead
of May 7.

The reason is pretty simple: we need three more sessions with
Carlos to map the second-shift handoffs, and his calendar has
been packed. I've now got those sessions locked in for next week,
so we're back on a known schedule.

Nothing about scope, budget, or final delivery date changes —
the back half of the project still completes on June 18 as
planned.

I'll send a brief summary after the Carlos sessions wrap so
you've got the updated picture without having to ask.

Thanks for the patience on this one.

Best,
Maya
```

Notice what Claude did: no over-apologizing, no corporate hedging, a clear new date, an explanation that doesn't blame anyone, and a forward-looking commitment. That came directly from the **Voice** and **Goal** lines in the brief.

Here's the technique at work — a writing-quality term you'll hear me use a lot: *constraint-driven generation*. By naming the constraints up front (audience, voice, outcome), you're not telling Claude *what* to write. You're telling Claude what counts as *good*. Big difference.

One more pro tip. Don't reinvent the brief every time. Keep a saved template — we'll build yours in Lesson 5 — and swap in the specifics. Maya now has three: client email, internal team update, prospect cold-outreach. She rotates them like cycling jerseys.

Next lesson, we're going to flip the script. Instead of *you* briefing Claude, you'll learn how to make Claude brief *you* — by getting it to ask the questions you didn't know to answer. See you there.

## Lesson 3: Flipping the Interview — Letting Claude Ask You

**Runtime:** ~10 minutes
**Learning objective:** By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the "interview flip" technique — asking Claude to ask you clarifying questions *before* drafting — to surface the missing context that makes the difference between mediocre and excellent output.

### Lesson Script

Welcome back. Today we're doing my favorite move in the entire course. It's the single most underused prompting technique I've seen in two years of teaching this stuff. And it takes one sentence to learn.

Here's the workday frustration I want to call out first. You sit down to draft a strategy memo, or a pitch, or a tricky team announcement. You stare at the blank screen. You don't even know what to put in the brief, because you don't know what you don't know. Sound familiar?

This lesson fixes that.

The technique is called the **interview flip**. Instead of you briefing Claude, you ask Claude to interview *you*. You put it in the role of a thoughtful colleague who, before they pick up the pen, wants to ask you a few questions.

Here's what that looks like in practice. The magic sentence is at the bottom:

```
You're helping me draft a strategy memo for my CEO.

Topic: Whether we should shut down our self-serve product tier
and focus 100% on enterprise.

Before you draft anything, ask me 5-7 sharp questions that you'd
need answered to write a memo that actually changes her mind one
way or the other. Then wait for my answers.
```

That last line — *"Then wait for my answers"* — is critical. Without it, Claude often fires off questions and then immediately tries to answer them itself.

Now, what does Claude do? Something like this:

```
Great — before I draft, here's what I'd want to know:

1. What's the current revenue split between self-serve and
   enterprise, and what's the trend over the last 4 quarters?
2. What's the gross margin on each tier? (Self-serve is often
   lower than people think once support and infra costs are
   loaded in.)
3. What's the strategic logic for keeping self-serve — is it a
   pipeline source for enterprise, a brand play, or just legacy?
4. What does your CEO already believe? Is she leaning toward
   shutdown, or do you need to overcome resistance?
5. What's the team impact — would shutting it down require
   layoffs, reorgs, or just reassignment?
6. What's the customer impact — how many self-serve customers
   would be affected, and is there a graceful migration path?
7. What's the timing pressure? Is this a Q3 decision, an annual
   planning input, or a board-meeting talking point?

Tell me what you can on each. If you don't know the answer to
one, just say "skip" and I'll work around it.
```

Stop. Look at those questions. *That's the brief you didn't know you needed to write.* If you'd tried to brief Claude cold, you would've forgotten three of those — probably the ones that matter most.

The technique at work here has a fancy name: **chain-of-thought elicitation through self-directed questioning.** In plain English: you're using Claude's ability to *think about what good looks like* to fill in the gaps in your own thinking. This is one of the published techniques in Anthropic's prompting guidance — they call it "thinking step by step."

Here's the workflow:

1. State the task in one or two sentences.
2. Ask Claude for 5–7 sharp questions before drafting.
3. Tell Claude to wait for your answers.
4. Answer them in plain language. Skip what you don't know.
5. Say "Now draft it."

That's the **Ask** step in our **Brief → Ask → Draft → Refine** pattern.

Two pro tips before we close.

First, calibrate the number of questions to the size of the task. Email? Three questions. Memo? Five to seven. Strategy doc or pitch deck? Ten to fifteen, and ask Claude to group them by theme.

Second, you're allowed to push back on a question you think is dumb or irrelevant. Just say "skip 3, that's not relevant." Claude will move on. This is collaboration, not a quiz.

The interview flip is the technique that, more than any other, separates "I tried Claude once" from "Claude is on my team." Practice it on every nontrivial task this week.

## Lesson 4: The Draft & Refine Loop

**Runtime:** ~10 minutes
**Learning objective:** By the end of this lesson, you will be able to refine a Claude draft through structured, targeted feedback — rather than starting over or accepting "good enough."

### Lesson Script

Here's a moment that happens to everyone. Claude produces a draft. It's pretty good. Maybe 70% there. There's something off about the third paragraph, and the closing falls flat, but you can't quite name what's wrong. So you do one of two things: you accept it and live with the rough edges, or you say "make it better" and Claude rewrites the whole thing into something *different* but not necessarily *better*.

Both moves are wrong. Today we fix that.

The **Refine** step in our **Brief → Ask → Draft → Refine** pattern is its own skill. It has two rules.

**Rule one: be surgical, not vague.** Don't say "make it better." Say "the third paragraph feels preachy — tighten it to two sentences and remove the word 'simply.'" Claude is excellent at targeted edits. It's mediocre at reading your mind.

**Rule two: refine in dialogue, not in a single shot.** Treat each Claude response as a draft you'd hand back to a colleague. Mark it up out loud. Ask for one or two changes at a time. The compounding effect is dramatic.

Let me show you the difference. Suppose Claude has drafted that client email from Lesson 2 — the one telling the COO about a one-week delay. You read it back. The opening is good. The middle is good. But the closing — "Thanks for the patience on this one" — feels small.

**Vending-machine refinement:**

```
Make it better.
```

You'll get a different email. Different subject line, different structure, sometimes different tone. You've thrown out the 70% that was working.

**Surgical refinement:**

```
Two changes:

1. The closing line "Thanks for the patience on this one" feels
   small for a slip notice. Replace it with one line that
   reinforces ownership without apologizing — something a
   confident operator would write.

2. The subject line is fine but slightly bland. Give me three
   alternates that signal "controlled update" rather than
   "problem incoming."
```

And Claude comes back with something like:

```
Updated closing:
"You'll have the new deliverable in your inbox May 14. I'll
flag any movement before then."

Three alternate subject lines:
1. Routing redesign — May 14 delivery (one-week shift)
2. Quick reset on the routing timeline
3. Routing project: updated delivery dates
```

Now you've kept everything that was already working and surgically improved the two things that weren't. That's two refinement cycles, maybe ninety seconds total.

The technique here — naming what you want changed and what you want preserved — is called **constrained editing**. It's the difference between a real collaborator and a magic 8-ball.

A few more refinement moves to keep in your back pocket:

- *"Tighten this from 200 words to 120 without losing the second paragraph."* — Length control with preservation.
- *"Same content, but in a more skeptical voice — less enthusiastic."* — Tone shift, content preserved.
- *"Give me three alternates for [specific sentence]."* — Variant generation on a single beat.
- *"Critique this draft as if you were [audience]. What lands? What feels off?"* — Meta-critique, then you decide what to act on.

That last one is especially powerful. Asking Claude to critique its own draft *from the perspective of the audience you defined in your brief* is one of the highest-leverage refinement moves there is.

Last tip. When you're done refining, ask Claude one final thing:

```
Read the final draft back. Is there anything I'd regret leaving
in?
```

This catches the small things — a corporate phrase that snuck back in, a number you should double-check, a tonal shift you missed. Claude is good at noticing its own slip-ups when explicitly invited to.

That's the loop. Brief, Ask, Draft, Refine. You now have all four moves.

## Lesson 5: Building Your Personal Prompt Playbook

**Runtime:** ~8 minutes
**Learning objective:** By the end of this lesson, you will have a small, personal playbook of 3–5 saved prompts you can reuse for the writing tasks you do most often — turning the four-step pattern into a Monday-morning system.

### Lesson Script

You've now learned the full pattern: **Brief → Ask → Draft → Refine.** You've watched it work on a LinkedIn post, a client email, and a strategy memo. The last question is: how do you turn this from *something you remember to do sometimes* into *the way you work*?

Answer: a personal playbook.

A playbook is just a small library of saved prompts — three to five to start — that match the writing tasks you actually do every week. Not the cool stuff you might do someday. The boring repeating stuff that already eats your time.

Here's how to build yours in fifteen minutes.

**Step one: Inventory.** Look at your sent folder, your Slack DMs, your Google Docs from the last two weeks. List the writing tasks that show up more than twice. Common ones for the operators I work with:

- Customer follow-up emails
- Internal team weekly updates
- Cold outreach to prospects
- Meeting recap notes
- Difficult conversation scripts (delays, pushback, no's)
- Tweet / LinkedIn post variants of an idea

Pick the three or four that consume the most time.

**Step two: Build a CAVG template for each.** Remember CAVG from Lesson 2 — Context, Audience, Voice, Goal. For each recurring task, write a reusable brief with placeholders for the parts that change. Like this:

```
You're helping me draft a customer follow-up email.

Context:
- I'm an account manager at [company]. The customer is [name]
  at [company]. We last spoke on [date] about [topic].
- The status is [where we left things].

Audience: [name + role + skepticism level + what they care
about].

Voice: Direct, warm, no corporate hedging. Slight dry humor okay.

Goal: [the specific outcome you want from them].

Before drafting, ask me 2-3 sharp clarifying questions.
Then wait for my answers.
```

Notice that I built the **Ask** step right into the template. That's the playbook trick — you bake the four-step pattern into the saved prompt so you don't have to remember it each time.

**Step three: Save them somewhere you'll actually find them.** Three options, in order of friction:

1. A simple Google Doc or Notion page titled "Claude Playbook." Free, frictionless, works today.
2. Claude's "Projects" feature (on the paid plan) — you can attach saved instructions to a project and start every conversation pre-loaded.
3. A snippet manager like TextExpander or Raycast — assign a shortcut like `;femail` to drop the template anywhere.

Most learners overcomplicate this. A Google Doc is enough.

**Step four: Refine the templates over time.** Every time a saved prompt produces something *almost* but not quite right, take 30 seconds to update the template. Add a missing piece of context. Tighten a vague instruction. Within a month, your playbook will be sharper than anything I could write for you, because it's tuned to *your* work.

Let me show you a worked example. Here's what one consultant's "Difficult conversation script" prompt looks like after six months of refinement:

```
You're helping me prep for a difficult conversation.

Context:
- The conversation is with [person + role + relationship to me].
- The topic is [the hard thing — be specific, including the
  uncomfortable parts].
- Stakes for me: [what I'm worried about / what I want to avoid].
- Stakes for them: [what they're likely worried about].
- Our prior pattern in tough conversations: [how it usually
  goes].

Voice: Mine is calm, direct, willing to sit in silence. Theirs
is [defensive / argumentative / shuts down / etc.].

Goal: Walk out with [the specific outcome] without damaging the
relationship.

Before drafting anything, ask me 4-6 sharp questions to surface
what I'm avoiding or assuming. Then wait for my answers.

When I say "draft it," produce:
1. A 3-line opener I can say verbatim
2. The 2-3 specific points I need to make, in order
3. The most likely pushback and a one-sentence response to each
4. A graceful close that names the next step
```

That prompt was a one-paragraph template six months ago. It's now a five-section workflow that handles 90% of her hardest meetings. *That's* a playbook.

So your assignment, before we wrap, is simple. Pick one recurring writing task you did this week. Turn it into a CAVG template with the Ask step baked in. Save it. Use it on Monday.

That's the whole course. You came in treating Claude like a vending machine. You leave treating it like the smartest colleague on your team — one you brief, interview, draft with, refine with, and eventually build a working rhythm with.

Welcome to the collaboration. I'll see you in the capstone.
